Looking at the graphs above that both show non-white population and African-American population, as well as the below graph showing where Superfund sites are located, there is a visible correlation between the location of superfund sites and race. The more diverse locations, especially the Chicagoland area, seem to also have the greatest amount of Superfund sites, demonstrating environmental injustices. Specifically, to the lower-middle an area of increased diversity seems to line up with superfund sites in both Madison and Montgomery County. Vermillion County and Winnebago County also demonstrate this trend. Additionally, after looking at median income, it is clear that income is not the main reason for the given trend, as even higher-income counties have quite a few superfund sites, once again visible looking at the Chicagoland area. This points to race being even more of an issue, since even with higher income having highly diverse areas correlates with superfund sites. Clearly, having the wrong skin color can leave you breathing the wrong air or drinkning the wrong water.
While not the ultimate evidence of environmental injustice, that superfund sites exist and are not being taken care of properly in Illinois is an issue not only close to home, but requiring immediate solutions. The environment may adapt, but when it comes to Superfund levels of pollution, the impact is likely to include disturbance to water supplies and the local ecosystem, which can be disastrous. Ultimately, these injustices close to home are something us residents of Illinois must advocate for change in, and as soon as possible.
